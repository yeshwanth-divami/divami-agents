# genHRX specs and Daksh improvements
Context: internal review of Daksh workflow automation, brownfield adoption, approvals, packaging, spec quality, and a parallel AI-feasibility request.
## Act I - Opening logistics and informal setup (~00:00 - ~00:23)
The meeting opened casually, with Yeshwanth joining from the kitchen and asking for the Daksh docs link to be shared so he could walk everyone through the latest process changes.
## Act II - Daksh V1 workflow improvements are introduced (~00:23 - ~00:29)
Yeshwanth framed the visible improvements as Jira updates, GitHub actions, role-specific commands, and stateful tracking, but insisted the deeper improvement was the jump in document quality rather than the automation layer itself.
> *"that is a minor improvement in my opinion compared to the quality of documentation that it started generating."*
> — Yeshwanth Reddy Yerraguntla _(~00:23:37)_
## Act III - Gates, approvals, preflight, and handbook updates are explained (~00:29 - ~00:34)
The walkthrough then drilled into role-based commands, approval metadata, preflight checks, time tracking, and handbook updates as part of the closing criteria, showing Daksh as a managed workflow rather than a loose skill bundle.
## Act IV - Change requests and brownfield onboarding become the real open design problem (~00:34 - ~00:42)
After the feature tour, the unresolved problem became how to bring ongoing projects into Daksh with minimal friction, especially when change requests have to propagate backward through vision, BRD, PRD, and TRD.
## Act V - Naveen reframes brownfield work around intent, not automation (~00:42 - ~00:50)
This was the first major pivot: Naveen argued that auto-retrofitting brownfield projects was the wrong hill to die on because the real blocker was whether teams actually believed in the process enough to clean up their projects.
> *"don't spend time on that... the main problem is not that there is no intent on the team to really use it."*
> — Naveen Puttagunta _(~00:42:57)_
## Act VI - Risk reporting beats hard approval blocking (~00:50 - ~01:03)
The room converged on a clearer model: keep approvals, but move the enforcement pressure into a risk report so missing approvals and inconsistencies are visible without turning the system into a rubber-stamp factory.
> *"I want I don't want approval to be a a blocker. Approval process should be there."*
> — Naveen Puttagunta _(~00:55:54)_
## Act VII - Jira flexibility and modular adoption keep Daksh usable in messy projects (~01:03 - ~01:12)
From there the discussion moved into making Jira optional and allowing Daksh adoption at a module level, so new work can be brought into process discipline even when the rest of a project is still inconsistent.
## Act VIII - Distribution and packaging strategy are debated (~01:12 - ~01:21)
The next debate was practical: whether Daksh should be distributed through cloning, copying, tagged bundles, or a lightweight installer, with the group leaning toward project-owned files instead of shared mutable checkouts.
## Act IX - Feedback on questions, UI specs, and prompt quality raises the quality bar (~01:21 - ~01:31)
Naveen pushed back on taking every complaint literally, defended the system asking many questions, and then shifted into a sharper critique that Daksh still needed much stronger solution-design, UI, backend, PRD, and TRD guidance.
## Act X - Multi-model document generation becomes the main process experiment (~01:31 - ~01:52)
The meeting then turned into a concrete PRD/TRD generation discussion: second and third opinions, synthesis across models, manual experimentation before over-automation, and a live-project training session instead of a toy demo.
> *"how can we perhaps bring in this... second opinion third opinion validations so that automatically it gets corrected"*
> — Naveen Puttagunta _(~01:31:49)_
> *"let's do it on a uh live"*
> — Naveen Puttagunta _(~01:44:07)_
## Act XI - The close shifts to feasibility, commitments, and follow-ups (~01:52 - ~02:11)
The ending split between action-item assignment and a separate feasibility thread on real-time audio/video emotion analysis, which Yeshwanth treated as a research problem with a Wednesday checkpoint rather than something ready for implementation.
> *"it's not mainstream... we need a lot lot of data."*
> — Yeshwanth Reddy Yerraguntla _(~02:05:41)_
