# Use Case Anatomy

A use case is a proof of competence disguised as a story. It is not a portfolio piece (screenshots with captions), not a blog post (thought leadership), and not a retrospective (internal). It is an outbound sales document wearing the clothes of a narrative.

---

## What It Proves

Three things, in this order:

1. **We understood the real problem.** Not the surface symptom — the deeper structural issue. This is the most important section. It shows diagnosis before prescription.

2. **We had a method.** The process was repeatable, not lucky. A client reading this thinks: "If they did this systematically for someone else, they'll do it for me."

3. **The outcome was measurable.** Without numbers, it's a testimonial. With numbers, it's evidence.

---

## Mandatory Sections

### 1. Context Seed (Opening)

3-5 sentences. A reader who has never heard of this client, industry, or project can follow the document. Seed the context — don't link to it.

Must answer: Who is the client (anonymized if needed)? What industry? What was at stake?

### 2. Situation

The world before the engagement. What was broken, slow, or missing? What business pain did this cause? Use the client's language — the words they used in kickoff calls, not your internal jargon.

This section earns the reader's trust. If the reader's own pain shows up here, they keep reading.

### 3. Approach

What you did and why. Not a feature list — a narrative of decisions. Why this tech stack? Why this methodology? What tradeoffs were made? What alternatives were considered and rejected?

Structure: the journey, not the destination. Show the thinking, not just the doing.

### 4. Outcome

Measurable results. Metrics first, qualitative impact second. Before/after comparisons. Business language: revenue, time saved, error reduction, user adoption — not "we shipped 47 components."

Every metric needs a source or gets flagged as unverified.

### 5. Key Decisions (Optional but Powerful)

A table of 3-5 pivotal decisions made during the project. What was decided, what the alternative was, and why. This section separates a use case from a case study — it shows judgment, not just execution.

### 6. Client Voice (Optional)

Direct quotes from the client. Attributed or anonymized. Real words beat polished prose.

**Qualifying test:** The quote must be testimony about pain experienced or outcome achieved. Meeting coordination, design feedback, and process summaries do not qualify — they tell the reader about your working relationship, not about the problem or the result. If no qualifying quote exists, omit the section entirely. An empty Client Voice section is better than a padded one.

### 7. Open Questions

Unresolved items: metrics pending verification, attribution needed for quotes, context gaps the user must fill. Never omit this section — write "None." if empty.

---

## What Does NOT Belong

- Internal process details (sprint velocity, team size debates)
- Technology for technology's sake ("we used Kubernetes" — unless the choice was the point)
- Self-congratulation or hollow adjectives ("cutting-edge", "world-class")
- Anything the client hasn't approved for external publication
